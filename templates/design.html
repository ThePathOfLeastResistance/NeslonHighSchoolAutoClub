Our Current Plans

We are currently modifying our current car to be faster by making a new section for the backend of the car to reduce weight and improve efficiency. In addition, we are also designing a new model and reaching out to companies for sponsorships.

Future Plans

In the future, we will build a newer model, which will have an aluminum frame. This means that the newer model will be much lighter and hence much faster. In addition, the new model will have 4 shock absorbers instead of 2, with suspension in the back and front of the vehicle. Furthermore, it will have a regenerative brake system, allowing us to put energy back into the battery when we break.

Overview

This is our second year participating in the Waterloo EV Challenge, we plan on modifying the car to be faster by reducing the weight. By next year's competition, we plan on having our aluminum vehicle done. Our current design includes three sections: the front, housing the steering and brake system, the body where the driver is located, and the rear, containing all the electric vehicle electronics, including the battery and controller.

The Front

Break System

We used a hydraulic brake system, which features a master cylinder on the left side of the driver connected to 3 hydraulic brake calipers, two for the front wheels and one for the rear wheel, using brake flex hoses. When the driver steps on the break, it turns a rod that pushes on the pins of the master cylinder, which stops the car.

Steering System

We implemented a rack-and-pinion steering system, connecting it to a rod via universal joints, linking to both the steering shaft and the steering wheel. When the wheel is turned, the shaft alters the wheel's direction in response to the input.

Body

The central section of our electric vehicle is where the driver is seated. This section has the harness, seat, and parking brake. In the competition, teams must rotate drivers every 15 minutes -- the change must be within 60 seconds-- and all vehicles have to be equipped with a five-point racing-style seat belt system.

Seating and Harness

For our seat, we repurposed a broken chair by cutting it to fit in the electric vehicle and securing it to the vehicle using screws. For our harness, we use a five-point harness.

Headrest

We manufactured our own headrest by gluing a sheet of foam to a wood board and wrapping it in a red cloth.

The Emergency Brake

We got the emergency brake from a broken car and hooked it up to our break system. However, after the brake is not as effective as we would have hoped.

Shock Absorber

This allows our wheel to be in constant contact with the road, which increases the traction and control. However, this added alot of unnecessary weight, which we plan on changing for our new model by using smaller and lighter shocks.

PM DC Motor

Our motor works with both 12 volts and 24 volts. However, the chain connecting the motor to the wheel was not properly tensioned, resulting in the chain falling off during the competition. However, we were able to attach a chain tensioner to the chain guard, fixing the issue.

Battery

The batteries that we used are YELLOWTOP® D34/78 - 120Amps, which is required for the compeition

Wiring

The wire that we used for most of the electrical connects is a 2 gauge wire, which we crimped ourselves.

Master Switch

When we turn on the vehicle, we first have to turn on the master battery disconnect switch, and only then can the driver turn on the car.

Controller

For our controller, we were using the KDS – Mini Brushed DC Series/PM Motor Controller, which worked with both 12v and 24 volts. This controller does not support a regenerative brake system, which is why we are buying a new controller.
